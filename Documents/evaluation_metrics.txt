Mitigating the Judge's Self-Preference Bias
Using a LLM as a judge introduces a severe "Self-Preference Bias" (SPB), where the model
systematically favors arguments that match its own generative style. To mitigate this in a
zero-shot environment:
●​ Black-Box Perturbations: Before passing the Proposer's and Critic's arguments to the
Judge, apply automated perturbations—such as synonym replacement or
paraphrasing—to neutralize stylistic differences and obfuscate the "authorship" of the
text.
●​ Cognitive Load Decomposition: Do not ask the Judge for a single, holistic score.
Instead, use a structured, multi-dimensional evaluation strategy that forces the Judge to
independently evaluate specific legal elements (e.g., "Rate the validity of the Rule cited,"
then "Rate the logical Application") to reduce cognitive overload and bias.
●​ Position Swapping: To prevent position bias, run the Judge evaluation twice for each
round, swapping the order of the Proposer's and Critic's responses in the prompt, and
average the normalized probability scores of the Judge's verdict.

Controlling Context Windows and Preventing Hallucination Loops
●​ Adaptive Sparse Communication: Instead of appending the full, verbose chat history
at every turn, gate interactions using a preference score that mixes the agent's
confidence with the "information gain" of the new turn. If a turn does not offer sufficient
new information, it is pruned from the context.
●​ Summary-Based Relay: Divide the debate into sub-stages where agents are forced to
produce condensed summaries of their arguments before passing them to the next
agent, significantly reducing token bloat.

Evaluation Metrics
●​ Information Gain (Cosine Dissimilarity): Measure the cosine dissimilarity between the
embeddings of consecutive agent responses. This mathematically verifies whether the
agents are actually debating (high dissimilarity/information gain) or just falling into a
repetitive agreement loop.
●​ Self-Preference Bias (SPB) Quantification: Create equal-quality pairs of responses
and measure the directional evaluative deviation to specifically track how often your
Judge defaults to its own stylistic baseline.
●​ Zero-Shot Stance Detection Accuracy: Measure how effectively the Judge can
synthesize the debate process and correctly identify the final legal stance compared to
strong zero-shot baselines.

●​ Structural Fidelity / Parsing Success Rate: Since you are relying on zero-shot ReAct,
track the exact percentage of turns where the models successfully output valid, parsable
tool-calling syntax without breaking character.
